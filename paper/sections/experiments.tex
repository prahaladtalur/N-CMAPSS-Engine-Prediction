\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

The controlled benchmark uses N-CMAPSS FD1 as the primary subset and FD2 as a cross-subset check. All headline runs use the same data pipeline described in Section~\ref{sec:methods}: last-1000 timestep truncation, train-fit feature standardization, asymmetric MSE with $\alpha=2.0$, Adam optimization~\cite{kingma2015adam}, and the shared callback stack. We assign stable row identifiers (T1, T2, \ldots) to the canonical results so that the discussion can refer to exact experimental records without repeating the full configuration each time. Tables and figures that report our own results cite these row identifiers for provenance; external comparison rows are cited to their source papers.

\subsection{FD1 headline results}

Table~\ref{tab:fd1} reports the controlled FD1 head-to-head. WaveNet is the strongest apples-to-apples FD1 model by RMSE (T1: 6.523), followed by CNN-GRU (T2: 7.006) and MSTCN (T3: 7.604). MSTCN is therefore not the FD1 winner under the most rigorous controlled protocol.

  with different protocols (full-flight and no-temporal, respectively).}
\label{tab:fd1}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llrrrr}

The FD1 result is a cluster result: dilated convolution, hybrid, and multi-scale attention models operate in the 6.5--7.6 RMSE band (T1--T3), while no-temporal and full-flight recurrent baselines are far worse (T8, T10). This supports the claim that attention/multi-scale temporal models are competitive on N-CMAPSS, while MSTCN's FD1 rank remains below WaveNet and CNN-GRU.

\subsection{FD2 robustness}

On FD2, MSTCN is the best model by RMSE among the available apples-to-apples rows (T11: 6.495), with WaveNet close behind (T12: 6.739) and CNN-GRU degrading under the more variable flight envelope (T13: 19.812). WaveNet still has the highest Accuracy@20 on FD2 (T12: 99.50 versus T11: 99.01). Thus, the FD2 result supports the ``top cluster'' framing for MSTCN without establishing dominance across every metric.

\subsection{Multiseed reproducibility}

To bound run-to-run variance we trained MSTCN over three random seeds (42--44)
and WaveNet over two seeds (45--46) on FD1 with the same apples-to-apples
protocol. Table~\ref{tab:multiseed} reports the per-seed mean. The MSTCN
mean RMSE of 8.140 ($\pm0.74$, n=3) and WaveNet mean of 8.238 ($\pm1.44$, n=2)
are both above the best-run figures in Table~\ref{tab:fd1} (T1: 6.523; T3: 7.604),
confirming that single-seed results reflect favorable draws. WaveNet's wider
standard deviation ($\pm1.44$) indicates higher run-to-run variability than
MSTCN ($\pm0.74$); the two models' multiseed means are statistically
indistinguishable given the sample sizes.

\subsection{Sequence-length evidence}

Table~\ref{tab:length} gives the available evidence behind the sequence-length discussion. The comparison is deliberately labelled as evidence rather than a complete ablation: the 1000-timestep rows are controlled head-to-head runs, the 250-timestep rows are shorter 10-epoch probes, and the full-flight rows come from older full-length baselines. The table still shows a clear practical pattern: the best 1000-timestep models are substantially stronger than the 250-timestep and full-flight rows, but the architecture and training differences prevent a causal sequence-length claim.

\subsection{External comparison}

The closest N-CMAPSS literature anchors are Solís-Martín et al.~\cite{solismartin2021stacked}, who report validation RMSE 6.24 using a 161-cycle window, and Cohen et al.~\cite{ncmapss_eventual2023}, who report RMSE 7.75 for ANN-Flux. Our controlled WaveNet FD1 result (T1: 6.523), controlled MSTCN FD1 result (T3: 7.604), and controlled MSTCN FD2 result (T11: 6.495) fall within this N-CMAPSS band. C-MAPSS-only results from STAR~\cite{fan2024star}, TTSNet~\cite{ttsnet2025}, and Xu et al.~\cite{xu2024msattn} are treated as architectural precedent rather than direct numerical baselines because the datasets and sequence regimes differ substantially.
