\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

The controlled benchmark uses N-CMAPSS FD1 as the primary subset and FD2 as a secondary cross-subset check. Unless otherwise noted, the apples-to-apples rows use the same data pipeline described in Section~\ref{sec:methods}: last-window truncation, train-fit feature standardization, reader max RUL 65, Adam optimization~\cite{kingma2015adam}, and the shared callback stack. The final controlled suite uses three seeds (42--44), with machine-readable metrics committed alongside the code. External comparison rows are cited only as contextual literature anchors, not as direct matched baselines.

\subsection{FD1 headline results}

Table~\ref{tab:fd1} reports the three-seed FD1 head-to-head at $T{=}1000$. WaveNet is the strongest and most stable model in this comparison (RMSE $6.868\pm0.299$). MSTCN is stable and competitive (RMSE $7.890\pm0.526$), but it does not beat WaveNet on FD1. CNN-GRU has one strong seed and two collapsed seeds, so its mean is much worse than its best run.

The FD1 result is therefore not a single-run leaderboard. WaveNet is robustly best among the three models tested here, MSTCN is a stable second cluster member, and CNN-GRU should not be described as a reliable top model despite its good seed-42 result.

\subsection{FD2 robustness}

On FD2, MSTCN is the best model by RMSE among the available apples-to-apples rows (T11: 6.495), with WaveNet close behind (T12: 6.739) and CNN-GRU degrading under the more variable flight envelope (T13: 19.812). WaveNet still has the highest Accuracy@20 on FD2 (T12: 99.50 versus T11: 99.01). Thus, the FD2 result supports the ``top cluster'' framing for MSTCN without establishing dominance across every metric.

\subsection{Multiseed reproducibility}

The three-seed suite was run specifically to bound run-to-run variance. Table~\ref{tab:fd1} shows that the ranking changes when seed variance is included: WaveNet remains strong, MSTCN remains stable, and CNN-GRU is not reproducible under the same settings. This demonstrates why architecture rankings should not rely on a single seed.

\subsection{Sequence-length evidence}

Table~\ref{tab:length} gives the clean sequence-length result requested by reviewers: one architecture (WaveNet), one training recipe, and three seeds. The sweep shows that short windows are effective across the tested range. The best mean is at $T{=}500$, with $T{=}250$ and $T{=}1000$ close enough that the result should be interpreted as a practical short-window regime rather than an exact optimum.

\subsection{External comparison}

The closest N-CMAPSS literature anchors are Solís-Martín et al.~\cite{solismartin2021stacked}, who report validation RMSE 6.24 using a 161-cycle window, and Cohen et al.~\cite{ncmapss_eventual2023}, who report RMSE 7.75 for ANN-Flux. Our three-seed FD1 WaveNet mean at $T{=}1000$ (RMSE $6.868\pm0.299$), the best matched window-sweep mean at $T{=}500$ (RMSE $6.690\pm0.173$), and the MSTCN FD1 mean (RMSE $7.890\pm0.526$) are in the same broad N-CMAPSS numerical band. In addition, Table~\ref{tab:sota-chase} reports the best single run found in a targeted seed sweep. WaveNet at $T{=}1000$ with seed 47 reaches RMSE 6.197 on FD1, slightly lower than the 6.24 N-CMAPSS anchor of Solís-Martín et al. Because the external rows use their own splits, inputs, and validation/test conventions, this is a repository benchmark result rather than a fully matched head-to-head claim. C-MAPSS-only results from STAR~\cite{fan2024star}, TTSNet~\cite{ttsnet2025}, and Xu et al.~\cite{xu2024msattn} are treated as architectural precedent rather than direct numerical baselines because the datasets and sequence regimes differ substantially.
