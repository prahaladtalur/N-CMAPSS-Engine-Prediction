\section{Ablations and Discussion}
\label{sec:ablations}

This section interprets the benchmark against the three paper claims. Several experiments needed for causal attribution are absent, so we separate supported observations from missing ablations.

\subsection{C1: sequence length}

The matched WaveNet sweep supports the directional claim that short operational windows are effective on N-CMAPSS. Across seeds 42--44, the mean RMSE values are 7.098 at $T{=}100$, 6.880 at $T{=}250$, 6.690 at $T{=}500$, and 6.868 at $T{=}1000$. The differences among 250, 500, and 1000 timesteps are small relative to the scale of the task, while 100 timesteps is slightly worse. The supported conclusion is therefore a short-window regime, not a proof that exactly 1000 timesteps is optimal.

The remaining limitation is that this sweep stops at 1000 timesteps and does not include 2000, 5000, or full-flight inputs for the same architecture. Older full-flight baselines are much weaker, but those rows still conflate length with architecture and training details. We therefore report C1 as evidence for short operational windows, corroborated by short-window N-CMAPSS challenge submissions~\cite{lovberg2021variable,solismartin2021stacked}, not as a complete full-flight ablation.

\subsection{C2: multi-scale temporal attention}

MSTCN is competitive but not uniformly dominant. In the final FD1 three-seed table, WaveNet has the best mean RMSE (6.868), MSTCN is second and stable (7.890), and CNN-GRU is unstable (17.168 mean RMSE because two seeds collapse). This contradicts any simple claim that MSTCN is the FD1 winner, but it supports treating MSTCN as a stable member of the stronger temporal-model group. On FD2, an older controlled run has MSTCN best by RMSE (T11: 6.495), ahead of WaveNet (T12: 6.739), but that FD2 observation has not yet been repeated with the same three-seed protocol.

The interpretation supported by the current results is that WaveNet is the robust FD1 winner among the tested models, MSTCN is a stable competitive model, and CNN-GRU should not be used as a reliable top baseline without additional tuning. The architectural attribution remains open. We have not yet ablated MSTCN's multi-scale branches, removed its global fusion attention, or matched parameter counts against WaveNet and Transformer. Those experiments are required before claiming that the multi-scale or fusion-attention components themselves cause the observed performance.

\subsection{C3: asymmetric loss}

The project uses asymmetric MSE as its default loss, and the three-seed WaveNet comparison now gives preliminary empirical support for that choice. At $T{=}1000$, asymmetric MSE obtains mean RMSE 6.868 and mean Accuracy@20 98.24, symmetric MSE obtains mean RMSE 7.214 and mean Accuracy@20 97.65, and asymmetric Huber obtains mean RMSE 8.904 with high variance because one seed collapses. The loss is also aligned with the asymmetric PHM scoring tradition~\cite{saxena2008damage} and with recent cost-sensitive RUL loss design~\cite{mpm2024loss}.

However, C3 is still preliminary. The comparison covers one architecture, one subset, one window length, and only three seeds. There is no $\alpha$ sweep over the late-penalty ratio and no architecture-wide loss comparison. We therefore frame asymmetric MSE as the best loss in this limited review-response suite, not as a general theorem about RUL loss design.

\subsection{Open experiments}

The next experiments are specific. Extending the matched WaveNet sweep to 2000, 5000, and full-flight inputs would test the C1 boundary. MSTCN variants with one dilation scale and with global fusion attention removed would test the C2 mechanism. Symmetric MSE, asymmetric MSE, and asymmetric Huber across multiple architectures, including an $\alpha \in \{1.0,1.5,2.0,3.0\}$ sweep, would turn C3 from a limited FD1 observation into a broader empirical comparison.
