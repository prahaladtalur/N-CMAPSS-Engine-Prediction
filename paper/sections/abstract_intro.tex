\begin{abstract}
Predicting remaining useful life (RUL) from turbofan sensor trajectories is a core benchmark problem in aerospace prognostics, but simulation benchmark results should be separated from claims about certified in-service use. The N-CMAPSS dataset~\cite{ariaschao2021ncmapss} combines a commercial engine simulation model with real recorded flight envelopes, yielding variable-length flights of $10^4$--$10^5$ timesteps per engine and heterogeneous operating conditions. We present a deep-learning pipeline for variable-length N-CMAPSS flights and compare recurrent, temporal-convolutional, WaveNet, and multi-scale attention families under matched preprocessing, training, and evaluation code. In a three-seed FD1 review-response suite, WaveNet is the strongest and most stable model at $T{=}1000$ (RMSE $6.868\pm0.299$), MSTCN is competitive but lower (RMSE $7.890\pm0.526$), and CNN-GRU is unstable because two of three seeds collapse. A matched WaveNet sweep shows that short windows are competitive across 100--1000 timesteps, with $T{=}500$ best by mean RMSE ($6.690\pm0.173$). In a separate SOTA-chase run, the best WaveNet seed reaches RMSE 6.197, $R^2=0.9175$, and Accuracy@20 99.71 on FD1, below the 6.24 N-CMAPSS validation RMSE anchor reported by Solís-Martín et al.~\cite{solismartin2021stacked}. Asymmetric MSE is modestly better than symmetric MSE in this suite (RMSE $6.868\pm0.299$ versus $7.214\pm0.559$), while asymmetric Huber is high variance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern turbofan datasets contain dense sensor suites whose readings, integrated over many cycles, encode gradual component degradation. Translating those recordings into a calibrated estimate of remaining useful life is the central task of data-driven engine prognostics. The cost structure of this task is asymmetric: conservative early predictions incur maintenance overhead, while late predictions are more serious in a safety-critical setting. The PHM-2008 scoring function~\cite{saxena2008damage} encodes this asymmetry directly and remains a common evaluation metric in the field. Our experiments study this problem in simulation data only and should not be read as evidence for operational deployment.

For more than a decade, deep-learning research on this problem has been driven by the synthetic CMAPSS benchmark~\cite{saxena2008damage}, where each engine produces a few hundred cycles of low-dimensional sensor data under a small set of operating conditions. The N-CMAPSS dataset of Arias-Chao et al.~\cite{ariaschao2021ncmapss} was introduced to close the gap between this benchmark and the conditions encountered in commercial operation: trajectories are simulated from real recorded flight envelopes spanning ascent, cruise, and descent, with sample-rate-level sensor data on the order of $10^4$--$10^5$ timesteps per cycle. The hybrid physics-augmented baseline of Arias-Chao et al.~\cite{ariaschao2022fusing} and the published submissions to the 2021 PHM Society Data Challenge~\cite{lovberg2021variable,devol2021inception,solismartin2021stacked} establish N-CMAPSS as the contemporary benchmark of record for turbofan RUL.

N-CMAPSS poses three difficulties that did not arise on CMAPSS. Flights are variable-length, with each engine yielding a different total cycle count and per-cycle sequence length. Operating conditions are heterogeneous within and across flights, so a single model must generalize across the full envelope. And the sequence regime is qualitatively different: at $10^4$--$10^5$ timesteps per cycle, naively ingesting full flights pushes recurrent and quadratic-attention models toward their failure modes for long inputs.

The methodological response to these difficulties has spanned four families. Recurrent networks such as LSTM~\cite{hochreiter1997lstm}, GRU~\cite{cho2014gru}, and bidirectional variants were the first applied. Temporal convolutional networks~\cite{bai2018tcn}, WaveNet-style dilated convolutions~\cite{oord2016wavenet}, and dilated/inception variants~\cite{lovberg2021variable,devol2021inception} were introduced to handle long inputs without sequential bottlenecks. Self-attention models, building on the Transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention} and long-sequence variants such as Informer~\cite{zhou2021informer}, have been adapted to RUL through hierarchical and two-stage designs~\cite{fan2024star,ttsnet2025}. Most recently, multi-scale TCN architectures with attention modules over the dilation hierarchy~\cite{xu2024msattn} have been proposed as a way to combine the long effective receptive field of TCNs with the cross-channel and cross-scale weighting of attention. The publicly reported benchmarks across these families are not directly comparable: most are reported on CMAPSS rather than N-CMAPSS, training protocols and input lengths vary substantially, and few studies report the same accuracy, RMSE, and PHM-score triple under a single harness.

This paper contributes a deep-learning pipeline for variable-length N-CMAPSS flights together with a shared registry of 22 architectures drawn from recurrent, convolutional, hybrid, transformer, and multi-scale attention families. The pipeline standardizes sequence preparation (last-$N$ truncation, per-feature standardization), loss choices, evaluation metrics (RMSE, $R^2$, PHM score, accuracy at $\pm 20$ cycles), and the experimental harness used for head-to-head numbers. The results support the following bounded claims:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1 (sequence length).} In a matched WaveNet sweep, short windows from 100 to 1000 timesteps all work well, with $T{=}500$ best by three-seed mean RMSE and $T{=}250$ close behind. The result supports short-window modeling, not a claim that exactly 1000 timesteps is optimal.
  \item \textbf{C2 (architecture stability).} WaveNet is the strongest FD1 model in the three-seed controlled suite. MSTCN is stable and competitive, but not dominant. CNN-GRU has one good seed and two collapsed seeds, so its single-seed rank is not reliable.
  \item \textbf{C3 (loss choice).} Asymmetric MSE slightly improves the three-seed WaveNet mean over symmetric MSE and asymmetric Huber in this setup, but the loss comparison remains preliminary because it covers one architecture, one dataset subset, and only three seeds.
\end{itemize}
